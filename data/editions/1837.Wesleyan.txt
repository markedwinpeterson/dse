EXPLANATIONS.
THE following Catalogue has been prepared for the convenience of the Librarian
and Students of the University. As the time which could be devoted to its prepara-
tion would not admit of a detailed account of the subjects, edition and other parti
culars of each work, which are necessary to a full and complete Catalogue, nothing
farther has been attempted than an enumeration of the titles, briefly stated as might
be-and alphabetically arranged, generally with reference to the subjects.
The capitals denote the case, the small letters the shelf, and the numbers, the po
sition on the shelf.
The library has been enriched by many valuable donations, only a few of which
can be here noticed.—The most valuable was made by Thomas Chapman, Esq. of
Camden, N. J. and is called, as a token of respect to the Donor, the Chapman Li-
brary of the Wesleyan University.-This portion of the Library, in number about
2000 vols. consists of all the books in cases D, E, & F, except such as are marked
with a t. There are among them 177 folios, and 121 quartos. Of these 1655 vols.
were fixed at a low price-one half of the amount was presented by Mr Chapman,
and the other half paid by the Trustees. The remainder of the 2000 volumes was
given with no reserve but the privilege of adding to the number. Among the latter,
is a splendid copy of the Antwerp Polyglot, in 8' folio volumes. That part of the
Catalogue distinguished by a §, numbering 375 volumes, was the Library of the late
John Summerfield, and presented to the University by his brother-in-law, James
Blackstock, Esq. of N. Y. Those distinguished by a *, are books presented by the
British Conference, valued at 100%. Other valuable presents have been made by Au-
thors and other individuals, of which more specific notice cannot be taken in this
place.

REGULATIONS
FOR THE
LIBRARY.
1. THE Library will be opened every Monday and Friday morn
ing at half past eight o'clock.
2. No Student is allowed to have out more than two volumes at
one time.
3. Each volume must be returned in two weeks, under a penalty
of 12 cents for every week's detention-and all damages done to
Books will also be assessed at the discretion of the Librarian. These
fines must be paid at the time of assessment, or the delinquent will
be debarred the privileges of the Library.
4. Any Student keeping out a book six weeks, without returning
it, will be charged with the volume, or the set, as the case may require,
at a price for which, in the judgment of the Librarian and President,
the work may be replaced.
5. No unregistered Book, no Folios or Quartos, and no translation
of a Classical Text Book, can be taken from the Library without
special permission.
6. Students are not permitted to carry a book from the Uni-
versity premises, except in those cases where they lodge out-and
then only to their own lodgings.
7. At the regular hours of opening the Library, those wishing to
take Books will not be permitted to enter the room within the coun-
ter; but will hand to the Librarian, a strip of paper containing his
own name, together with the number and letters of the Work wanted;
and the Book will be handed him at the counter.


## p. 50 (#56) ##############################################

50
8. No Officer is permitted to take out a Book, unless it is charged
to him.
9. Any responsible person belonging to the Town, can have the
same privilege of the Library with the Students, by paying ten dol-
lars
per annum, in advance.
10. Each Wednesday afternoon in Term time, from two until five
o'clock, the Library, as well as the other Public Rooms of the Uni-
versity, will be open to the public.
N. B. As it is a tax upon the Officers, for persons to call at any
and all times, to visit the Public Rooms, it is hereby respectfully re-
quested of our citizens, especially, that they would call for that pur-
pose, as far as possible, only on Wednesday, as above.
